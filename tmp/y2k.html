Y2K and the Power Grid

by Rick Donaldson, CET, NÏNJY

 

FOREWORD: Y2K - A term heard almost every day now, used to describe variously the upcoming end of the world to the turn of the century.  Y2K, as used in this article will be used to describe a specific day, and the associated date-dependant computer programs, firmware and software that can and probably will be affected by the date change.  A little fast refresher course on Y2K for anyone that has no clue what this is all about.  Many years ago, programmers didn't have gigabytes of hard drive space or megabytes of random access memory in their computers.   They programmed in machine code, bits and bytes and assembly language.  Every byte of code was important  (8 bits is a byte, one byte is equal to one letter on your screen, or one number for purposes of explanation).  There was no extra space, no "upgrading memory", or any such things we do today.  To conserve space, programs were written in a manner that removed certain characters. Dates in this case took on dates like 01/01/99.  Computer CMOS (the stuff that cause your computer to wake up and start working) had this problem.  Today, you can still see the conservation of characters in some UNIX commands, SMTP connection processes (for instance, the HELO, instead of HELLO) and so forth.  The problem DOES exist, regardless of whether you believe it will cause serious problems or not.  Y2K in this light, is a real situation that must be dealt with.  Read on.

About four or five years ago, the term "Y2K" began impinging upon our senses.   At first, there were those that took it seriously.  As time went on, it became a joke among nearly everyone in business, government and even the personal computer world.   A small, but hardy band of individuals took up the hew and cry of Y2K however, and kept the term alive.  Constantly warning of the coming problems. They have been ridiculed, ignored and largely been made the butt of jokes. Groups of survivalist communities have formed, virtual communities over the internet have gotten together to help one another.

Today, less than a year from D-Day, 1 January 2000, the United States Senate (http://www.senate.gov/~y2k/documents/report/index.html)  has released their own study of how Y2K will affect the country.  Companies have jumped, perhaps too late, onto the bandwagon to repair computer systems.  Governmental agencies and departments are individually working toward a common goal of "becoming y2k compliant".  Power companies all over the country are trying to prepare for what may happen.  Every day, more and more information is coming out on national news programs regarding how this group or that company is preparing. 

In my own wanderings through the virtual community of the internet, I've discovered three types of people where Y2K is concerned. The first type are those who believe this is "The End of the World As We Know It" (TEOTWAWKI in some circles).  The next group are those who are absolutely ADAMANT that nothing is going to happen and they can't possibly see how a little billing error is going to cause major sociological upheavals.   The final group is, like nearly every other subject broached in the world, the majority.  They tend to fall in the middle of both the other groups, tend to be the majority and tend to believe something is going to happen, albeit, very likely nothing serious.  Until very recently, I personally fell into the last group.   As a computer professional, working with everything from unix, NT and Novell to Windows95 systems I felt that most systems would either be ok, or they are inherently non-critical systems that even IF affected won't be damaged or cause anything serious to happen.  However, over the course of researching this problem, discussing it with other computer professionals and trying to understand the intricacies of interconnected systems, my own conclusions have changed drastically. 

Now, don't get me wrong, I still do not believe that the end of the world is coming - at least not due to Y2K.  The world is too complex however, to completely discount the chances that massive computer glitches will reduce this planet to ashes.   Computers are insinuated into every part of everyday life in Western society.   They are in our cars, our microwaves, our remote controls on our televisions.   They are in our fire and burglar alarm systems, they control the power grids of the United States (and other countries). They control communications systems, satellites, cellular telephones and global positioning systems.  Computers can be found in your wrist watch, stereo system and our telephone systems.  Computers control the gas pumps, cash registers, billing and banking, air traffic control, train systems across the country.  Computers control our traffic control systems in our cities. If you can name something, computers are probably in some way, connected now. 

Do we really know what is going to happen, when January 1st rolls around?  I can say, without a doubt that the answer to this questions is unequivocally the answer is ... NO.  We do not know for certain.  The reasons we do NOT know are simple.   First, most people have only recently become interested in learning about y2k.   Companies in many cases have determined that spending the research money would not be beneficial to them, therefore, they have not seriously investigated the problem. Now, many of them are trying to make a determination of what to do, how it will affect them and so on.  This leaves little time to fix any problems they do happen to discover.

Second, the government themselves, while attempting to take the problem seriously from the beginning simply didn't organize themselves.  Every department or agency within the government has direction that they will become y2k compliant. However, there is no concerted effort to do so.  All organizations have had to form their own internal groups to address the situation, sometimes without proper training in the field.  In my own work as a contractor with the government, it has been very difficult to locate, speak to, or get information from the various y2k groups.  Most are unwilling, or unable to give accurate information regarding the status of a particular organization.   Everyone is keeping the real information close to their chests, or, worse really do not know.

Third, we have little or no experience with multiple computer failures, across the country at the same time.  When something of this magnitude occurs, there is very little previous history to go on, from which to make decisions, and from which we can make valid educated guesses as to the outcome.  This simply means, we are playing this game blindly. 

With that said, understand that everything that happens from the point you read this article, forward is dynamic.  Time only will tell the truth of what might or will happen.  For the rest of this article, based on educated guesses and real-time information, we will cover the most likely scenario.

The Power Grid

After examining the available information on the power grid, and speaking to some "officials" at the local power company (mostly Public Relations folks, since no one else would deign to speak with us lowly public) we have determined that the number one, most dangerous scenario is the Power Grid Scenario.  The power grid is basically broken into three sections in the US. The entire state of Texas is one area of the grid.   The western grid is considered to be inclusive of and west of the states of Colorado, New Mexico, Wyoming, and Montana. The eastern grid includes all states to the east of those listed above.  The power "grid" is a term used to describe the interconnection between various local utility companies, larger government facilities, commercial and co-op facilities.  All of these include such things as nuclear power plants, hydroelectric plants, fossil fuel plants and the smaller, less used air and solar plants of the United States.

Figure 1: From Senate Report

Each plant supplies, on demand the amount of power required to run its particular region.  If power is not used by that region, it may be sold to another region based on the higher requirements somewhere else.  In other words, if your area doesn't have a high use of current at a particular time of the day, but an adjacent area is in a heatwave and everyone is using their air conditioning, that other area will use much more power.  Hence, your area will send (or more accurately, power will be PULLED FROM your area) current to the other area.  This is all controlled by interconnected computer systems, controlled by internally embedded chip sets.  It is the "embedded" chips that are the most important in this scenario.  There are literally thousands of such processors on circuit boards of most large, controlling systems (such as the power grid computers).  These are not our familiar personal computers as many people think. 

Embedded chips are difficult to find because generally you do not program the small computer boards. They are already programmed.  They are difficult to access with terminals most of the time, and use what is known as "firmware", computer programs on read-only memory chip sets.  These programs would need to be tested carefully for y2k compliance, and this is the root of the problem. It is difficult at best, to test almost un-checkable hardware, when there are thousands of them everywhere.

Go back a couple of years, about April or May of 1997.  I do not recall the exact date now, but many people will recall about two years ago, on a Saturday or Sunday about 11 or 12 o'clock Mountain Time, the power went out.  Not just to my city of Colorado Springs, but to the entire state of Colorado (with a couple of minor exceptions).  I got on the ham radio and discovered this.  At the time it happened, we assumed it was simply the military base where I happened to be pulling my weekend duty in the Reserves.   As I checked, it turned out that multiple states were involved.  Although I never heard the full story on how many places were affected, power was off for several hours.  A small animal had been killed attempting to go through a power panel.   This precipitated a chain reaction that eventually engulfed at least ten states.   If you look at Figure 1, above, you will note that all of the states on the western grid were affected.  Amazingly, there are about ten states there. Count them yourself.

If one small region is affected, a cascade effect will be felt throughout the given grid area.  This means that the power will start to shut down over several states in a matter of minutes.  It may come back up, slowly as companies attempt to restart their area of the grid.  Or it may remain offline while each attempts to communicate with the other regions.  Regardless of what happens next, the chances are extremely good that power will NOT be restored quickly.  Here is why. Remember the embedded chip sets we mentioned?  If those are not completely and total removed and replaced with chip sets that have been tested for Y2K compliance, they can not and will not be replaced very quickly.  With thousands of them to be replaced, the amount of man-power required to do so will simply be much more than the utilities can handle. 

We say, therefore, that the most likely affect of Y2K will be the loss of the power grid, on both sides of the country and for the most part, nearly all of the grid will drop offline.  There will be some independents that can work alone, for a time, until their fuel source runs out.  Nuclear power plants will likely be Y2K compliant, however, they can not supply more than 20% of the nations power.  They can not, therefore supply everyone, nor even some of the regions for more than a short time.   Expect if the power goes out, then, that there will be rationing, power outages and on-off-on cycles for hours, perhaps days after 1 January 2000.

The Senate report states quite clearly, "Of particular concern is that, with only a little over a year to go, 34% of the firms are operating without a written plan.  In addition, the assessment phase is only 75% complete (federal agencies are 99% complete with this phase). Remediation and testing is only 36% complete.   Given that Y2K experts contend that between 40 and 70% of the total effort will be expended in testing along, there may not be sufficient time to complete this."

In layman's terms, this means "no concerted effort" to head off the impact of imbedded chip set problems.  No concerted effort.  No one is working with anyone else in order to fix this problem before it becomes a disaster.   Ok, enough said, I think you get the point now.

Essentially, we believe that there will be a problem with the power grid.   The American public has become so dependent upon the use of electricity in everyday lives that the loss of the power grid will affect us all deeply, and in many ways. If we lose the grid the following situations will rear their ugly heads. Remember that this comes in the winter time.

1)  Lack of heat in areas affected by cold weather.  Death by freezing for those unfortunate enough not to have any other form of heat.

2)  Mobility of the community.  Lack of power means lack of pumping gas. You can't get to work without cars, trains and mass transportation unless you live close to your job. In this day and age, most people live a minimum of several miles from their jobs.

3)  Panic in cities.  After a few days, panic, rioting and looting will likely begin.  Gangs will begin to roam the streets taking what they wish.  The police forces of most cities will be hard-pressed to handle this situation.  Remember their communications will be up and down, as their own private power generators run out of fuel after a few days.

4)  Stores will lose food to lack of being able to keep it frozen.  

5) Hospitals have emergency power, at least many do.  If they lose commercial power, they go onto internally generated power.  However, eventually, even they will run out of fuel.

6) Communications. General telephone communications will be affected. Cell phones will be out.  Computers won't connect to the internet.  The internet is effectively gone.  (I know this upsets many hackers I've spoken to.  They seem to misunderstand the magnitude of this situation and can't understand, as one said to me a few evenings ago, "I can't see how a few billing problems will bring the world to a screeching halt.")

After having gone through the Y2K situation, a possible scenario with the power grid and some possible outcomes after the grid goes down, we can say only one thing will definitely come to pass.  That is, January 1st, 2000 will indeed come and go. Whether that date will simply be the change of the date from the "late 1900's" to the "early 2000's" or a "day that will live in infamy" (to steal another well known term) remains to be seen.  Our advice is simple.  PREPARE.   Prepare for the worst, hope for the best and if the best occurs, you've lost nothing.  If the worst occurs, at least you took the time to protect your family, your home, and your own lives against what might happen.
