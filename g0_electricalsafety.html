Module 10: Electrical and RF Safety

Subelement G0 — RF Exposure, Electrical Hazards, and Lightning Safety

By Rick Donaldson, NØNJY — n0njy@qsl.net

10.1 Introduction

Safety is not just an exam topic — it is the foundation of responsible amateur radio operation. This module covers RF safety (protecting yourself and others from radio frequency energy) and electrical safety (protecting yourself from lethal voltage and current). Both are tested on the General exam and are relevant every time you operate.

10.2 RF Safety Basics

Radio frequency energy is non-ionizing — it heats tissue rather than breaking chemical bonds. The eyes and testes are particularly susceptible to RF heating because they have limited blood flow to dissipate heat. RF safety concerns are most significant between 30 and 300 MHz, where the human body absorbs energy most efficiently. The FCC establishes Maximum Permissible Exposure (MPE) limits that vary by frequency. Limits for the uncontrolled (general public) environment are five times stricter (lower) than for controlled (occupational) environments.

10.3 RF Exposure Evaluation

Amateur stations above specified power thresholds must perform an RF exposure evaluation. Factors include:

  • Transmit power (watts)
  • Antenna gain
  • Duty cycle (percentage of time actually transmitting)
  • Distance to areas where people may be exposed
  • Frequency of operation

Duty cycle note: FT8 and RTTY (transmitting continuously during the TX period) receive the most conservative treatment. SSB voice has a lower duty cycle and is treated less strictly.

10.4 Electrical Safety

The “one-hand” rule: When working on energized circuits above 30 volts, keep one hand in your pocket or behind your back. This prevents current from flowing across your chest (heart) if you contact a live circuit.

Capacitor discharge: Large filter capacitors in power supplies can store lethal charges even when power is disconnected. Always discharge capacitors through a resistive bleeder load before working on a power supply.

High-voltage supplies: Tube amplifiers may use 2,000–5,000V DC. Treat these with extreme respect.

10.5 Lightning and Tower Safety

Lightning: Disconnect antenna feedlines from equipment during electrical storms. Install quality lightning arrestors at the point where feedlines enter the building. A properly installed grounding system provides the primary path for lightning current to dissipate safely.

Tower climbing: Always use a harness, lanyard, and appropriate fall arrest system. Never climb a tower without proper fall protection equipment. OSHA standards apply to amateur antenna tower climbing.

Module 10 Practice Questions

Q1 (G0A01) — What is one way that RF energy can affect body tissue?

  1. It ionizes atoms and molecules
  2. It heats body tissue
  3. It interferes with nerve impulses
  4. It causes bone density reduction

Q2 (G0B01) — What is the maximum electrical voltage at which the “one-hand” rule applies?

  1. 12 volts
  2. 30 volts
  3. 50 volts
  4. 120 volts

Q3 (G0A05) — Which is most important for an amateur operator to know about RF exposure?

  1. The maximum distance from which RF can be received
  2. The power density in areas where people may be exposed
  3. The total amount of RF transmitted per day
  4. The frequency of operation in relation to band plan allocations

Q4 (G0B11) — Which is an important consideration when installing an antenna near power lines?

  1. Antenna radiation pattern may be affected by the power line
  2. The power line may cause interference on the amateur band
  3. An antenna that falls can cause the power line to arc and injure or kill a person
  4. Operating near power lines requires a special FCC permit

Q5 (G0A03) — How can you determine whether the RF levels produced by your station are acceptable?

  1. By using a grid dip meter to measure the RF field strength
  2. By calculating the expected power density using FCC-provided worksheets
  3. By monitoring the SWR at the transmitter output
  4. By asking your neighbors if they notice interference

Q6 (G0B05) — Which of the following is a danger from lead-acid storage batteries?

  1. Electric shock from the battery terminals at charging voltage
  2. Explosive gas can be released when charging
  3. Battery acid is harmless if skin contact occurs
  4. Connecting a load to a fully charged battery may cause it to explode

Q7 (G0A08) — Which is the FCC’s threshold for when an amateur station must perform an RF exposure evaluation?

  1. All amateur stations are always required to perform an evaluation
  2. Stations that operate above specified power levels must perform an evaluation
  3. Only stations transmitting into wire antennas require evaluation
  4. Stations that use automatic control require evaluation

Q8 (G0B09) — What precaution should be taken when working on a power supply?

  1. Remove the antenna connection before working on the power supply
  2. Always work with both hands to ensure good contact
  3. Discharge filter capacitors before working on the circuit
  4. Use only rubber gloves when replacing filter capacitors

Answer Key

  1. B — RF energy heats body tissue (thermal effect)
  2. B — The one-hand rule applies at voltages above 30 volts
  3. B — Power density in exposed areas is the critical factor
  4. C — A falling antenna can cause a power line to arc, causing electrocution or fire
  5. B — FCC worksheets calculate expected power density for exposure evaluation
  6. B — Charging lead-acid batteries releases hydrogen gas, which is explosive
  7. B — Stations above specified power levels must perform an RF exposure evaluation
  8. C — Always discharge filter capacitors before working on a power supply
