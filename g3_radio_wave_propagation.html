Module 3: Radio Wave Propagation

Subelement G3 — Ionosphere, Solar Cycle, Skip, and NVIS

By Rick Donaldson, NØNJY — n0njy@qsl.net

3.1 Introduction

Understanding propagation separates a skilled HF operator from someone who simply turns on the radio and hopes for the best. HF signals travel vast distances because they interact with the ionosphere — a region of the upper atmosphere that can refract radio waves back to Earth. Learning how the ionosphere works, what controls it, and how to predict propagation conditions is one of the most satisfying technical aspects of amateur radio.

3.2 The Ionosphere

The ionosphere extends from approximately 60 km to 1,000 km altitude where solar radiation ionizes gas molecules, producing free electrons that affect radio wave propagation.

D Layer (60–90 km)

Exists only during daylight. The D layer absorbs HF signals rather than refracting them. This is why the lower HF bands (160, 80, and 40 meters) are essentially useless during the day for long-distance contacts. At night, the D layer disappears and those signals can reach the higher F layer.

E Layer (90–150 km)

Also forms during daylight and mostly disappears at night. Can support propagation for distances of 1,000–2,000 km. Sporadic-E (Es) is an anomalous condition in which dense patches of ionization form in the E layer, enabling propagation on frequencies up to 150 MHz, including VHF. Sporadic-E is unpredictable but common in late spring and early summer.

F Layer (150–500 km)

The primary layer for long-distance HF propagation. During daylight, the F layer splits into F1 and F2 sub-layers. At night they merge. The F2 layer at its peak can support propagation around the entire globe. The F layer persists at night because recombination of ions is slower at higher altitudes.

3.3 The Solar Cycle

Solar activity follows an approximately 11-year cycle. The solar flux index (SFI) measures the sun’s radio emissions at 10.7 cm wavelength. Higher SFI means better ionospheric ionization and better HF propagation on higher frequencies. An SFI of 150 or above generally indicates excellent 10- and 15-meter conditions.

The K-index and A-index measure geomagnetic activity. A K-index of 0–1 indicates calm conditions favorable for propagation. A K-index of 4 or higher indicates a geomagnetic storm, which can severely disrupt HF propagation at high latitudes.

3.4 Skip Distance and MUF

When a signal is transmitted at a low angle, it travels upward to the ionosphere and is refracted back to Earth. The area between the transmitter and the nearest point where the signal returns is the skip zone — an area unreachable by either ground wave or sky wave.

Maximum Usable Frequency (MUF): The highest frequency that will be refracted by the ionosphere for a given path. Signals above the MUF pass through the ionosphere and are lost to space. Operating near but below the MUF gives the strongest, most consistent signals.

3.5 NVIS Propagation

Near Vertical Incidence Skywave (NVIS) is a technique designed for short-range HF communication — typically 0 to 300 miles (0–500 km). Instead of low-angle radiation, NVIS uses nearly vertical radiation. The signal radiates almost straight upward, hits the ionosphere, and falls nearly straight back down. The skip zone is virtually eliminated.

NVIS is ideal for regional emergency communications. Effective frequency range is 3.5 to 10 MHz. The antenna for NVIS is a low dipole — mounted 1/8 to 1/4 wavelength above ground. A dipole at 8–15 feet above ground works well for NVIS on 40 meters.

  • Daytime NVIS: 7–10 MHz
  • Nighttime NVIS: 3.5–5 MHz

3.6 Other Propagation Modes

Transequatorial propagation (TEP): Occurs when signals cross the geomagnetic equator. Most common in spring and fall, primarily affecting the 6-meter band. Can enable contacts of 10,000+ miles.

Meteor scatter: Radio waves reflect off ionized trails left by meteors. Primarily a VHF/UHF mode. Contacts require high-speed digital modes due to brief burst durations.

Tropospheric ducting: Temperature inversions in the lower atmosphere create a refractive duct. Signals can travel hundreds of miles at VHF/UHF. Common over water and in coastal areas.

Aurora propagation: During geomagnetic storms, signals can reflect off the aurora borealis. Produces a distinctive buzzing or hissing signal quality.

Module 3 — Practice Questions

Q1 (G3A01) — Which of the following are good indicators of approaching solar maximum?

  1. Fewer sunspots and lower solar flux
  2. Increasing numbers of sunspots and higher solar flux
  3. Decreasing geomagnetic activity
  4. More stable ionospheric conditions

Q2 (G3B07) — What is NVIS propagation?

  1. Long-distance propagation by HF signals refracted from the outer atmosphere
  2. Propagation by signals reflected from the Moon
  3. Short-distance HF propagation using high elevation angles
  4. Propagation by VHF signals through the ionosphere

Q3 (G3C01) — Which of the following frequencies is most likely to be useful for NVIS propagation?

  1. 50 MHz
  2. 28 MHz
  3. 14 MHz
  4. 3.5 to 10 MHz

Q4 (G3A04) — Which of the following does a high K-index indicate?

  1. Good short-wave propagation conditions
  2. High geomagnetic activity
  3. Low solar flux
  4. Absence of ionospheric layers

Q5 (G3B05) — What is the maximum usable frequency (MUF)?

  1. The minimum frequency that produces useful propagation for a given path
  2. The highest frequency that will be refracted by the ionosphere for a given path
  3. The frequency with the lowest signal absorption in the D layer
  4. The frequency used to determine the lowest skip distance

Q6 (G3C05) — Which propagation type is most likely to allow 6-meter communication between stations 10,000 miles apart?

  1. Sporadic-E
  2. Transequatorial
  3. Meteor scatter
  4. Tropospheric ducting

Q7 (G3A12) — What is the effect of a sudden ionospheric disturbance on HF propagation?

  1. HF propagation is temporarily improved at high latitudes
  2. HF propagation is temporarily enhanced due to increased ionization
  3. HF propagation is blocked on the sunlit side of Earth
  4. HF propagation is permanently disrupted

Q8 (G3B03) — Why is the F2 region mainly responsible for the longest long-distance propagation?

  1. Because it is the densest of the ionospheric layers
  2. Because it exists only at night, reducing absorption
  3. Because it is the highest ionospheric region and persists day and night
  4. Because it absorbs the fewest radio signals

Answer Key — Module 3

  1. B — Increasing sunspots and higher solar flux indicate solar maximum
  2. C — NVIS uses high elevation angles for short-distance HF communication
  3. D — 3.5 to 10 MHz is the effective NVIS range
  4. B — A high K-index means high geomagnetic activity, often disrupting propagation
  5. B — The MUF is the highest frequency refracted by the ionosphere for a given path
