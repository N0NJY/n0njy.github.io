Module 9: Antennas and Feed Lines

Subelement G9 — HF Antennas, Coax, Ladder Line, Baluns, and SWR

By Rick Donaldson, NØNJY — n0njy@qsl.net

9.1 Introduction

Antennas and feed lines are arguably the most important topic for an HF operator. You can have the best transceiver on the market, but a poor antenna will defeat it. This module covers HF antenna types, feed lines, impedance matching, and standing wave ratio.

9.2 Fundamental Antenna Concepts

Half-wave dipole: The most fundamental HF antenna. Feed-point impedance approximately 72 ohms in free space. Length (feet) = 468 / f(MHz).

Quarter-wave vertical: Fed against a ground plane. Feed-point impedance approximately 36 ohms over a perfect ground. Length (feet) = 234 / f(MHz). Requires at least four radials for the ground plane.

9.3 Yagi Antennas

A Yagi uses a driven element plus parasitic elements (reflector behind, directors in front) to concentrate the radiation pattern in one direction. A 3-element Yagi achieves approximately 7–8 dBi gain (≈5–6 dBd). Front-to-back ratio of a well-designed 3-element Yagi may reach 20–25 dB.

9.4 Feed Lines

Coaxial cable: Standard feed line for amateur antennas. Most common impedance: 50 ohms. Loss increases with frequency. High SWR increases line loss.

Parallel / ladder line: Very low loss even at high SWR — typically less than 1 dB/100 ft. Cannot be routed through walls or near metallic objects. Used with antenna tuners for multi-band operation.

9.5 Baluns

A balun (BALanced-UNbalanced) connects a balanced antenna (dipole) to unbalanced feedline (coax). Without a balun, common-mode current on the outside of the coax shield causes RF in the shack, distorted radiation patterns, and interference. A 1:1 current choke balun is generally the best choice for a dipole. A 4:1 balun transforms 200 Ω to 50 Ω (or 300 Ω to 75 Ω).

9.6 Standing Wave Ratio (SWR)

SWR is the ratio of maximum to minimum voltage along a mismatched transmission line. SWR of 1:1 is perfect. Most amateur transceivers begin reducing output power when SWR exceeds approximately 3:1. High SWR on a long, lossy coax feedline causes significant power loss in the feedline itself.

Answer Key

  1. B — An antenna tuner matches feedline impedance to the transceiver’s 50-ohm output
  2. C — A half-wave dipole has approximately 72 ohms feed-point impedance in free space
  3. D — A multi-element Yagi provides more gain than any single-element antenna
  4. A — Antenna gain is the increase in signal strength in the desired direction vs. a reference
  5. B — Feed-point impedance decreases as the dipole is lowered toward 1/4 wavelength above ground
  6. B — 50 ohms is standard for amateur radio coaxial cable
