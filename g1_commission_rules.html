Module 1: Commission's Rules

Subelement G1 — Frequency Privileges, Power Limits & Regulations

By Rick Donaldson, NØNJY — n0njy@qsl.net

1.1 Introduction

You earned your Technician license by mastering a set of rules that govern VHF and UHF operations. General Class upgrades your privileges significantly — most importantly, it opens the HF spectrum to you. But with those privileges come new rules, and the FCC expects you to know them before you transmit.

This module covers the regulations most relevant to General Class operation: frequency privileges, power limits, third-party traffic rules, and special operating situations such as remote and space station communications.

1.2 General Class Frequency Privileges

The General Class license gives you access to portions of all amateur HF bands, plus everything a Technician can use. However, certain frequencies within each band are reserved exclusively for Amateur Extra Class licensees. You need to know where you can and cannot operate.

160 Meters (1.8–2.0 MHz)

General Class operators have full privileges on 160 meters: 1.800–2.000 MHz. This band is used primarily for domestic contacts and is heavily influenced by noise at lower latitudes. It is largely a nighttime band.

80 Meters (3.5–4.0 MHz)

  • Phone (SSB): 3.800–4.000 MHz (General)
  • CW and Digital: 3.525–3.600 MHz (General)
  • Extra only: 3.600–3.800 MHz for phone; 3.500–3.525 MHz for CW

40 Meters (7.0–7.3 MHz)

  • Phone (SSB): 7.175–7.300 MHz (General)
  • CW and Digital: 7.025–7.125 MHz (General)
  • Extra only: 7.125–7.175 MHz phone; 7.000–7.025 MHz CW

30 Meters (10.1–10.15 MHz)

All licensees above Technician share this narrow band. Power is limited to 200 watts PEP. No phone (voice) is permitted on 30 meters — CW and digital only. The ITU allocates 30 meters to fixed services as well, so amateurs must share and not cause interference.

20 Meters (14.0–14.35 MHz)

  • Phone (SSB): 14.225–14.350 MHz (General)
  • CW and Digital: 14.025–14.150 MHz (General)
  • Extra only: 14.150–14.225 MHz phone; 14.000–14.025 MHz CW

20 meters is the backbone of DX operation for most amateur operators. It is often open around the clock during periods of high solar activity.

17 Meters (18.068–18.168 MHz)

General and Extra share full privileges on 17 meters. Like 30 meters, there is no sub-band division between license classes — General licensees have access to the entire band.

15 Meters (21.0–21.45 MHz)

  • Phone (SSB): 21.275–21.450 MHz (General)
  • CW and Digital: 21.025–21.200 MHz (General)
  • Extra only: 21.200–21.275 MHz phone; 21.000–21.025 MHz CW

12 Meters (24.89–24.99 MHz)

Like 17 meters, the 12-meter band is shared equally by General and Extra Class. Full band access for General.

10 Meters (28.0–29.7 MHz)

  • Phone: 28.300–29.700 MHz (General)
  • CW and Digital: 28.000–28.300 MHz (General, with Extra exclusive below 28.025 MHz)

Note that the 10-meter phone segment includes FM operation at 29.600 MHz.

Key Memory Aid: The phrase “No New Privileges” applies to 6 meters and above. All your new General Class privileges are on HF — below 30 MHz.

1.3 Power Limits

The standard maximum power limit for amateur stations is 1,500 watts PEP output. This applies to most HF bands for General Class.

Exceptions you must know:

  • 30 meters: Maximum 200 watts PEP
  • Near certain government stations: Reduced power may be required
  • Novice/Technician subband segments: 200 watts maximum when operating in those segments

General Class operators are required to use the minimum power necessary to carry out the desired communication. This is a fundamental principle of amateur radio, not merely a regulatory suggestion.

1.4 Third-Party Communications

Third-party communications involve passing messages to or from persons who are not licensed amateur operators.

Domestic third-party traffic: Amateur operators in the United States may handle third-party communications freely within the US. A non-licensed person may speak directly over a station’s microphone, provided a licensed amateur is present and in control of the station at all times.

International third-party traffic: The US has third-party agreements with a limited number of countries. You may only pass international third-party traffic to a country that has a formal agreement with the United States. Passing third-party traffic to a country not on that list is a violation of FCC rules.

Station control requirement: The control operator is always responsible for the proper operation of the station, even when a third party is transmitting. You cannot walk away and leave a third party alone at the mic.

1.5 Remote Control and Automatically Controlled Stations

Remote control means controlling a station from a location other than the station itself. This is increasingly common with internet-linked rigs.

  • The control operator must be capable of terminating transmissions immediately if necessary
  • The station must comply with all applicable regulations as though the operator were physically present

Automatic control applies to stations that can operate without a control operator on duty — beacon stations and repeaters being the prime examples.

1.6 Space Stations and Earth Stations

The FCC defines a space station as an amateur station located more than 50 km above Earth’s surface. Any licensed amateur may transmit to a space station if they have the privileges necessary for the frequency being used. The club callsign for the ISS is NA1SS.

  1. C — 7.250 MHz is in the General phone segment of 40 meters
  2. C — 1500 watts PEP (exception: 200W on 30 meters)
  3. A — 200 watts PEP maximum on 30 meters
