Module 2: Operating Procedures

Subelement G2 — HF Voice, CW, Nets, DX, and Digital Operating

By Rick Donaldson, NØNJY — n0njy@qsl.net

2.1 Introduction

General Class opens up HF, and HF operating has its own culture, protocols, and procedures. Technicians are accustomed to FM repeater operation — push the button, talk, release. HF is different. You will encounter single sideband (SSB) voice, CW, digital modes, DX operating, nets, and emergency communications. Knowing the correct procedure for each mode and situation keeps the bands functional and professional.

2.2 HF Voice Operation — SSB

Single sideband is the dominant mode for HF voice. Unlike FM, there is no carrier — just a single sideband of the modulated signal. This improves efficiency and reduces bandwidth.

Which Sideband?

  • Upper Sideband (USB): Used on 20, 17, 15, 12, and 10 meters, and above
  • Lower Sideband (LSB): Used on 40, 80, and 160 meters
  • General rule: Frequencies 14 MHz and above use USB; below 14 MHz use LSB

This convention is not legally required, but it is the universal standard. Deviating from it causes confusion and interference.

Calling CQ

A typical CQ call: “CQ CQ CQ, this is November Zero November Juliet Yankee, NØNJY, calling CQ and standing by.”

Keep CQ calls brief. Three repetitions of CQ followed by your callsign once or twice is standard. Excessively long CQ calls are considered poor operating practice.

2.3 CW Operating — QSK

QSK (Full Break-In): A CW operating mode where your receiver opens between every dit and dah. This allows you to hear signals — or interference — during the gaps in your own transmission. QSK is considered the superior CW operating technique. The downside is increased mechanical wear on older relays.

Semi-break-in: The receiver opens only between complete characters or words. Less wear on the rig, but you can miss signals during your transmission.

2.4 Net Operations

A net is a scheduled meeting of amateur stations on a specific frequency for a specific purpose. Nets may be directed (controlled by a Net Control Station) or informal.

Net Control Station (NCS): The NCS runs the net, determines the order in which stations check in, and directs traffic. Other stations should follow NCS instructions.

Priority traffic: Emergency and priority traffic always supersedes routine net traffic. If you have emergency traffic while in a net, announce it immediately.

2.5 DX Operating

DX means communicating with distant stations, typically in other countries.

  • Do not call a DX station when it is specifically calling another region (“NA only” etc.)
  • Do not transmit on the DX station’s transmit frequency — they are often operating split
  • Listen for the DX station’s operating instructions before calling

Split operation: The DX station transmits on one frequency and listens on another range. If the DX says “listening 5 to 10 up,” you transmit 5–10 kHz above their transmit frequency.

2.6 Digital Modes

PSK31: Phase-shift keying at 31.25 baud. Very narrow bandwidth (31 Hz), excellent for keyboard-to-keyboard contacts in marginal conditions.

FT8: Developed by Joe Taylor (K1JT) and Steve Franke (K9AN). Uses 15-second transmit/receive cycles, 50 Hz bandwidth, and can decode signals 20 dB below the noise floor. FT8 has become dominant on HF because it works when no other mode is practical.

RTTY: Uses FSK at 45.45 baud with 170 Hz shift. Still widely used in contests.

Winlink / VARA: Used for sending formal email-style messages through the amateur network. Common in ARES/RACES emergency communications.

2.7 Emergency Communications

ARES (Amateur Radio Emergency Service) is organized by the ARRL. RACES (Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service) is organized with local and state governments. Both serve disaster communications needs.

In emergency operations, use plain language — not codes or abbreviations the served agency may not understand. Clear and concise communication is paramount.
