Module 5: Electrical Principles

Subelement G5 — Impedance, Reactance, Resonance, Power, and Decibels

By Rick Donaldson, NØNJY — n0njy@qsl.net

5.1 Introduction

The General Class exam includes a meaningful number of questions on electrical theory. You covered basic DC theory for your Technician license. Now you go deeper into AC circuits, impedance, resonance, and power — the foundations for understanding antenna systems, transmission lines, and transceiver circuits.

5.2 Impedance and Reactance

Resistance (R): Opposes current and dissipates energy as heat. Constant regardless of frequency.

Inductive Reactance: XL = 2πfL — Increases with frequency.

Capacitive Reactance: XC = 1/(2πfC) — Decreases with frequency.

Impedance: Z = R + jX, magnitude |Z| = √(R² + X²). Measured in ohms.

5.3 Resonance

A resonant circuit has its inductive and capacitive reactances equal and canceling. Resonant frequency: f = 1/(2π√LC). In a series resonant circuit, impedance is at minimum at resonance. In a parallel resonant circuit, impedance is at maximum at resonance. Q factor measures the sharpness of the resonance: Q = XL/R.

5.4 Transformers and Baluns

A transformer uses mutual inductance to transfer energy between windings. Turns ratio: Vs/Vp = Ns/Np. Impedance transformation: Zs/Zp = (Ns/Np)². A balun (BALanced-UNbalanced) connects a balanced antenna (dipole) to an unbalanced feedline (coax). A 1:1 current balun reduces common-mode current on the coax shield.

5.5 Power and PEP

True power P = E × I × cos(θ). Power factor PF = cos(θ). PEP (Peak Envelope Power) is the average power during one RF cycle at the peak of the modulation envelope. The FCC measures transmitter power as PEP.

5.6 Decibels

dB = 10 × log10(P2/P1)

Key values:

  • +3 dB = power doubled
  • −3 dB = power halved
  • +10 dB = power × 10
  • +20 dB = power × 100
  • +30 dB = power × 1000
  • 0 dBd = 2.15 dBi

Module 5 Practice Questions

Q1 (G5A01) — What is impedance?

  1. The rate of energy transfer in a resonant circuit
  2. The opposition to the flow of current in an AC circuit
  3. The opposition to the flow of current in a DC circuit
  4. The force that produces voltage in a circuit

Q2 (G5B01) — What dB change represents a two-times increase or decrease in power?

  1. Approximately 6 dB
  2. Approximately 3 dB
  3. Approximately 10 dB
  4. Approximately 1 dB

Q3 (G5A03) — What is the unit of impedance?

  1. Volt
  2. Ampere
  3. Ohm
  4. Watt

Q4 (G5C01) — What causes a voltage to appear across the secondary winding of a transformer?

  1. Capacitive coupling between the windings
  2. Mutual inductance
  3. Resistive coupling through the core
  4. Electrostatic charge buildup

Q5 (G5B03) — How many watts are consumed by a 12-VDC light bulb drawing 0.2 amperes?

  1. 2.4 watts
  2. 60 watts
  3. 0.024 watts
  4. 24 watts

Q6 (G5A07) — What happens when load impedance equals internal source impedance?

  1. Maximum voltage is delivered to the load
  2. Maximum current is delivered to the load
  3. Maximum power transfer occurs
  4. No power is delivered to the load

Q7 (G5B09) — What is the RMS voltage of a sine wave with a peak value of 17 volts?

  1. 12 volts
  2. 17 volts
  3. 24 volts
  4. 8.5 volts

Q8 (G5A11) — What is inductive reactance?

  1. The opposition to DC current flow in an inductor
  2. The opposition to AC current flow due to the inductance of a coil
  3. The amount of energy stored in a capacitor
  4. The resistance of the wire in an inductor

Answer Key

  1. B — Impedance is the total opposition to AC current flow
  2. B — 3 dB represents a doubling or halving of power
  3. C — Ohms are the unit of impedance
  4. B — Mutual inductance causes the secondary voltage
  5. A — P = E × I = 12 × 0.2 = 2.4 watts
  6. C — Maximum power transfer occurs when source and load impedances are equal
  7. A — RMS = Peak × 0.707 = 17 × 0.707 ≈ 12 volts
  8. B — Inductive reactance is the opposition to AC current due to inductance
