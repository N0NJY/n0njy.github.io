Module 7: Practical Circuits

Subelement G7 — Receivers, Transmitters, Power Supplies, and Filters

By Rick Donaldson, NØNJY — n0njy@qsl.net

7.1 Introduction

This module connects theory to hardware — actual circuit configurations found in amateur radio equipment. You will learn how receivers, transmitters, and power supplies work internally, plus filter basics.

7.2 Superheterodyne Receiver

The superheterodyne (superhet) design is the standard for virtually all HF receivers. The incoming RF signal is mixed with a local oscillator (LO) to produce an intermediate frequency (IF). All filtering, amplification, and detection occurs at this fixed IF, making it practical to build sharp, stable filters regardless of receive frequency. Image frequency: A second undesired frequency will also mix with the LO to produce the same IF. Pre-selector filters must reject the image before the mixer. SDR (Software Defined Radio): Replaces IF hardware with digital processing. Wide-bandwidth waterfall displays show all band activity simultaneously.

7.3 SSB Transmitter Stages

  1. Microphone amplifier
  2. Balanced modulator — produces DSB-SC signal
  3. Sideband filter — selects one sideband
  4. Mixer/VFO — translates to transmit frequency
  5. Driver amplifier
  6. Final power amplifier (PA)
  7. Low-pass filter — removes harmonics

ALC (Automatic Level Control): Prevents overdriving the transmitter. Excessive ALC action compresses audio and causes distortion. The ALC indicator should swing on voice peaks but not remain pegged continuously.

7.4 Power Supplies

Full-wave bridge rectifier: Four diodes producing pulsating DC at twice the input frequency. Large electrolytic capacitors filter the pulsating output.

Linear regulator: Maintains constant output by dissipating excess voltage as heat. Inefficient but very clean output.

Switching (switch-mode) power supply: Converts DC to high-frequency AC, transforms, rectifies, and filters. Very high efficiency. Most modern amateur power supplies are switching types.

7.5 Filters

  • Low-pass: After the PA to remove harmonics
  • High-pass: To block AM broadcast interference
  • Bandpass: IF filters in receivers
  • Notch (band-reject): Removes a single interfering carrier
  • Crystal filter: Very high Q using quartz crystals. Forms the sharp IF filters in HF receivers.

Answer Key

  1. C — PIV across each diode in a bridge equals the peak output voltage
  2. B — A product detector combines the IF with the BFO to produce audio
  3. C — A microprocessor replaces complex digital circuitry
  4. C — Full-wave rectifier output consists of positive pulses at twice the input frequency
  5. B — A good SSB signal is free of spurious emissions
  6. B — The product detector multiplies the IF signal by the BFO to produce audio
  7. B — Switching power supplies are far more efficient than linear types
  8. B — De-emphasis restores flat audio response, complementing transmitter pre-emphasis
